# **Strategic Analysis of Italian Real Estate Holding Structures for Canadian Investors: The S.r.l. Model**

The following report provides a detailed examination of the legal, fiscal, and administrative frameworks governing the establishment and operation of a *Società a Responsabilità Limitata* (S.r.l.) in Italy for the purpose of holding residential real estate. This analysis is specifically tailored for Canadian residents who are non-resident in Italy, accounting for the unique challenges presented by international reciprocity agreements, corporate governance requirements, and the Italian anti-avoidance tax regime as of 2026\.

## **Strategic Summary and Operational Overview**

The utilization of an Italian S.r.l. for holding single residential properties provides a robust mechanism for risk isolation and scalable asset management within the Eurozone. This structure separates the liabilities associated with property ownership—such as construction contracts, supplier disputes, and third-party claims—from the personal assets of the Canadian shareholders.1 However, the current legal climate, influenced heavily by Canadian domestic property restrictions and the resulting Italian response, makes the pre-incorporation phase critical for project viability.3

### **Critical Red Flags for Canadian Shareholders**

1. **Reciprocity Restrictions:** Due to the Canadian ban on foreign buyers (effective until 2027), Italian notaries currently restrict Canadian residents from purchasing residential properties in major urban areas (CMAs). Investment is largely limited to rural municipalities with fewer than 10,000 inhabitants unless specific residency or dual-citizenship criteria are met.3  
2. **Società di Comodo (Non-Operating) Status:** If the S.r.l. does not generate sufficient rental income—specifically at least 4.75% of the property's value annually—it will be classified as a "convenience company." This triggers a 34.5% effective IRES rate and prevents the deduction of various costs or the use of VAT credits.13  
3. **The Banking Barrier:** While a resident director is not legally required, the vast majority of Italian traditional banks will refuse to open corporate accounts for companies where all directors and shareholders are non-resident in the EU. This operational reality often necessitates the appointment of a local representative or the use of sophisticated digital banking intermediaries capable of handling Italian tax payments.15

## **The Principle of Reciprocity and Legal Capacity for Canadians**

The threshold question for any Canadian investor is the principle of reciprocity established under Article 16 of the Italian Civil Code. This article dictates that foreign citizens may enjoy civil rights in Italy only if their home country grants equivalent rights to Italian citizens.3

The implementation of the Canadian *Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act* on January 1, 2023, has profoundly impacted this balance.3 Because Canada has restricted foreign acquisitions in urban centers, Italy has applied reciprocal limitations on Canadian citizens who do not hold an Italian residence permit or EU citizenship.3

Under current interpretations for 2026, a Canadian-owned S.r.l. (or a Canadian individual) is generally permitted to acquire residential property only in Italian municipalities with fewer than 10,000 residents, provided these are not considered satellite communities of larger metropolitan areas.11 A property in the historical center of Florence, Rome, or Milan is likely inaccessible to a non-resident Canadian through a new S.r.l. unless the investor can demonstrate an exception, such as dual citizenship or a pre-existing preliminary contract signed before the restrictions took effect.4

The notary (*Notaio*) serves as the state’s gatekeeper in this regard and is legally obligated to verify the existence of reciprocity before executing the deed of sale. If reciprocity is absent, the transaction is null and void, and the land registry will refuse to record the title.20

## **Chronological Steps for S.r.l. Incorporation**

The formation of an Italian S.r.l. is a highly regulated process that cannot be completed entirely online without professional intermediation. For Canadian investors, the process typically spans 4 to 8 weeks, depending on the speed of document legalization in Canada.5

### **Phase I: Preparatory Documentation and Tax ID**

The foundational step involves obtaining an Italian tax code (*Codice Fiscale*) for each shareholder and prospective director.15 For Canadian residents, this is managed through the Italian Consulate in Canada or by granting a Power of Attorney (POA) to a local professional in Italy who can apply at the *Agenzia delle Entrate*.7 If the shareholder is a Canadian corporation rather than an individual, the corporation itself must also obtain an Italian tax code.15

All Canadian corporate documents—such as Certificates of Incorporation and Board Resolutions authorizing the Italian investment—must be translated into Italian and legalized with an Apostille to be recognized by the Italian notary.1

### **Phase II: The Act of Incorporation**

The formal birth of the company occurs when the shareholders or their proxies sign the Deed of Incorporation (*Atto Costitutivo*) and the Bylaws (*Statuto*) before an Italian notary.1

* **Procura Speciale:** Most Canadian investors utilize a "special power of attorney" to avoid traveling to Italy for the signing. This document must be executed in Canada and Apostilled.7  
* **Share Capital Deposit:** For an S.r.l. with a single shareholder, 100% of the share capital must be deposited upfront.5 For multiple shareholders, 25% is the legal minimum for cash contributions.7 To expedite the process, these funds are often deposited into the notary’s dedicated escrow account rather than a temporary bank account, as the latter can take weeks to open for non-residents.15

### **Phase III: Registration and Activation**

Within 20 days of the signing, the notary files the deed with the Register of Companies (*Registro delle Imprese*).5 Once registered, the company receives its VAT number (*Partita IVA*) and its unique identification number.15 The final step in this phase is the activation of the Certified Email address (*PEC*) and the digital signature for the legal representative, which are mandatory for all official business communications in Italy.15

### **Phase IV: Banking and Financial Operations**

Opening a permanent corporate bank account is frequently the most difficult step for a Canadian-owned entity.15 Most banks require the director to visit a branch in person for an interview and to sign the KYC (Know Your Customer) forms.16 Without an active bank account, the company cannot pay property taxes (*IMU*) or utility bills via the required electronic formats, effectively stalling operations.16

## **Annual Obligations and Ongoing Maintenance**

The Italian regulatory environment requires continuous administrative activity. Even a "passive" holding company is subject to the same accounting standards as an active trading business.2

### **Corporate and Tax Compliance Cycle**

The S.r.l. must maintain "ordinary" accounting, meaning every transaction—from a utility payment to a rental receipt—must be recorded in chronological order and verified against bank statements.2

### **Estimated Annual Maintenance Costs**

A standard property-holding S.r.l. should budget between **€4,500 and €8,000 per year** for professional services.5 This includes the fee for the *Commercialista*, who handles all tax filings and the electronic invoicing system, and the cost of the registered office, which must be a physical or virtual address in Italy.5

Audits (*Revisione Legale*) are generally not required for single-property holding companies unless they exceed significant thresholds: €4 million in assets, €4 million in revenue, or 20 employees for two consecutive years.25 Most residential holding entities will remain well below these limits.

## **Director Residency and Legal Representation**

Under Italian law, the management of an S.r.l. can be entrusted to a sole director or a board of directors.15

### **The Non-Resident Director Model**

There is no legal requirement for an Italian S.r.l. director to be a resident or a citizen of Italy or the EU.1 A Canadian resident can legally serve as the sole director of the S.r.l..1

* **Eligibility:** The director must be over 18, have full legal capacity, and not be disqualified by bankruptcy or criminal conviction.27  
* **Digital Domicile:** As of January 2025, every director with management powers must have their own personal Certified Email address (PEC) registered with the Companies Register.28 This PEC cannot be the same as the company's PEC.28

### **Complications and Risks**

While legally permissible, a structure where the director is 100% resident in Canada creates significant operational friction.

1. **Banking Access:** As previously noted, the lack of a local director is the primary reason banks refuse to open accounts.15  
2. **Public Authority Interaction:** In the event of an inspection, tax audit, or legal dispute, a local representative is often required to appear before administrative offices.37  
3. **Digital Signature Logistics:** The director must hold a digital signature kit. If this kit fails or expires, the company is effectively paralyzed until a new one is issued, which often requires physical or video identification.15

Many Canadian firms address these issues by appointing a "Nominee Director" or a "Special Proxy" in Italy who has limited powers to handle banking and local administration while the Canadian owners retain control over major financial decisions.16

## **Pitfalls and Tax Traps for Foreign Shareholders**

Navigating the Italian fiscal system as a foreign owner requires an understanding of several anti-avoidance measures designed to prevent the use of companies for "passive" purposes.

### **The Società di Comodo (Non-Operating Company) Rules**

The most significant trap is the *Società di comodo* regime.9 If a company holds assets like real estate but does not generate a minimum level of revenue, it is deemed to be "non-operating".13

* **The Revenue Formula:** For residential properties, the company is expected to generate revenue equal to at least 4.75% of the asset's value.14 For other real estate, the threshold is 6%.14  
* **The Penalty:** A company classified as *di comodo* must pay tax on a "deemed minimum income" regardless of its actual performance.13 Furthermore, the IRES rate is increased by a 10.5% surcharge, bringing the total corporate tax to 34.5%.13

### **The "Look-Through" Rule for Capital Gains**

The 2023 Budget Law introduced Article 23(1-bis) of the TUIR, a "look-through" rule for real-estate-rich companies.41 If a Canadian resident sells their shares in an Italian S.r.l. whose value is more than 50% derived from Italian real estate, Italy reserves the right to tax that capital gain at a flat rate of 26%.13 This effectively removes the tax advantage of selling a company instead of selling the underlying asset.41

### **Dividend Withholding and Treaties**

When the S.r.l. distributes profits to its Canadian shareholders, a 26% withholding tax generally applies.7 While the Canada-Italy Tax Treaty may offer a reduction in this rate, the administrative process to claim treaty benefits is complex and requires the shareholder to provide a Certificate of Residence from the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA).3

### **Value-Added Tax (VAT) Recovery**

Companies generally cannot recover VAT paid on the purchase or maintenance of residential properties if they are used for standard residential leases, as these are considered "exempt" activities for VAT purposes.13 This means the 10% or 22% VAT paid during acquisition is a final cost and cannot be offset against other company taxes.13

## **Strategic Questions for the Italian Commercialista**

Before finalizing the investment structure, the following questions should be addressed during the initial consultation with a tax advisor.

1. **Reciprocity and the CMA Threshold:** "Based on our Canadian city of residence, does the notary for this specific property in \[City/Village\] consider the 'Condition of Reciprocity' met for a residential acquisition?" 22  
2. **Società di Comodo Mitigation:** "What is our exact minimum revenue threshold for this specific property to avoid the *Società di comodo* penalties, and what are the specific IRES/IRAP implications if we do not rent it out?" 13  
3. **Banking and Proxy Solutions:** "Which banks in your portfolio will accept a Canadian-directed S.r.l., and what are the annual costs for an Italian resident 'Special Proxy' to handle the mandatory F24 tax payments?" 16  
4. **VAT Classifications:** "If we renovate the property, can we classify it as a 'tourist accommodation unit' to allow for VAT recovery on construction costs, and what are the resulting licensing requirements?" 13  
5. **Look-Through Capital Gains:** "If we eventually sell the S.r.l. quotas (shares) instead of the property, how do we apply the Article 23(1-bis) look-through rule to our Canadian tax reporting?" 41  
6. **Director Social Security:** "Does the Canadian director's residency exempt the company from paying Italian social security (INPS) contributions under the bilateral treaty, and what forms are required?" 3  
7. **Digital Domicile Compliance:** "Will you act as the intermediary for the mandatory personal PEC registration for each Canadian director to ensure we meet the 2025 deadlines?" 28  
8. **UBO Trust Disclosure:** "If our Canadian entity is held via a family trust, what level of disclosure is required for the Italian Register of Beneficial Owners (UBO), and are these names public?" 34  
9. **Inheritance and Gift Tax:** "How does holding property through an S.r.l. affect the 4-8% Italian inheritance tax rates for Canadian beneficiaries compared to direct individual ownership?" 43  
10. **Electronic Invoicing Logistics:** "What platform will the company use to receive electronic invoices from contractors, and how do we ensure we don't miss payments for utilities received via the SDI system?" 15

The establishment of an S.r.l. for real estate holding represents a long-term commitment to the Italian administrative and fiscal landscape. While it offers superior risk isolation and a platform for professional property management, the costs of compliance and the complexity of non-resident governance require meticulous oversight by Italian-based professionals.15 By addressing the reciprocity and *società di comodo* risks at the outset, Canadian investors can ensure their Italian holdings remain a viable and efficient component of their global portfolio.
