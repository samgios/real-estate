# **Comprehensive Risk Management and Insurance Protocols for Italian Residential Assets: From Construction to Short-Term Rental Operations**

The acquisition and development of residential real estate in Italy by international investment groups, specifically through a corporate structure such as the *Società a Responsabilità Limitata* (S.r.l.), presents a complex array of risk management requirements. For Canadian investors seeking to transform Italian properties into premium short-term rentals (STR), the insurance landscape is divided into two distinct chronological phases: the renovation or construction period and the subsequent operational phase. Each phase is governed by specific articles of the Italian Civil Code, regional administrative laws, and the evolving regulatory framework for tourism. This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the insurance products, statutory obligations, and financial considerations necessary to safeguard assets held within an Italian special purpose vehicle (SPV) by non-resident shareholders.

## **The Renovation and Construction Phase: Statutory and Contractual Protections**

The transformation of a residential property in Italy—whether through a full-scale build or a significant renovation—is a highly regulated process that shifts substantial liability onto the owner, acting as the *Committente* (Principal). Under Italian law, insurance during this phase is not merely a risk mitigation tool but, in many cases, a legal prerequisite for site opening and subsequent asset transfer.

### **Contractor’s All Risks (CAR) Policy**

The primary insurance instrument during the works phase is the Contractor’s All Risks (CAR) policy. This policy provides a "global shield" against accidental damage to the works themselves and liability for incidents occurring on-site.1 While standard in the construction industry, its application to private residential renovations is governed by the contract between the S.r.l. and the construction firm.

A CAR policy is fundamentally divided into two sections, each serving a critical function for the corporate owner:

* **Section A: Material Damage.** This section covers the physical structure under construction or renovation. It provides protection against fire, explosion, atmospheric events, and human error during execution.1 For a renovation project, it is essential to distinguish between the "new works" and the "pre-existing structure." Standard CAR policies cover the value of the new works, but the S.r.l. must ensure that the "pre-existing" building is also covered, particularly if structural changes could jeopardize the integrity of the original walls or foundation.1  
* **Section B: Third-Party Liability (RCT).** This protects the S.r.l. and the contractor from claims for bodily injury or property damage caused to third parties in connection with the works.2 It is crucial to note that under Italian law, employees of the contractor are not considered "third parties" for Section B purposes. Their protection falls under *Responsabilità Civile verso Operai* (RCO), which covers injuries or deaths of workers on-site.2

The arrangement of the CAR policy is a point of negotiation. In private contracts, the owner (the S.r.l.) may choose to purchase the policy directly—known as a "Principal-led" CAR—to ensure that all subcontractors are covered and that the S.r.l. remains the primary beneficiary in the event of a total loss.3

### **The Posthumous Decennial Policy (RC Postuma)**

A unique and critical component of Italian construction law is the *Polizza Decennale Postuma* (Posthumous Decennial Policy). Established under Article 1669 of the Civil Code and further regulated by Legislative Decree 122/2005, this policy mandates a ten-year warranty against structural collapse or "grave defects" that impair the property's habitability.1

While historically required for new builds, the regulatory update via Decree 154/2022 has extended the obligation of the decennial policy to "heavy" renovations (*ristrutturazioni pesanti*)—specifically those requiring a new static test (*collaudo statico*) and affecting the building's stability.6 For an S.r.l. owned by Canadian investors, obtaining this policy is essential if the property is intended for future resale or if the renovation is significant enough to trigger these legal requirements. Insurers typically mandate the presence of a technical controller (*controllo tecnico*) throughout the renovation phase to perform course-of-works inspections; without these documented inspections, obtaining the decennial policy at the end of the project is exceedingly difficult.8

### **Liability of the Committente (The S.r.l. as Owner)**

The S.r.l. cannot entirely delegate safety and liability to the construction firm. Under Italian law, the *Committente* (owner/principal) retains a "duty of vigilance." If an accident occurs due to poor site organization or failure to verify the contractor's credentials, the S.r.l. and its administrator can be held civilly and, in some cases, criminally liable.10 Therefore, an *RC Committente* policy—often included as an extension to a CAR policy or held as a standalone—is vital. This policy protects the S.r.l. from the consequences of its own presumed negligence in managing the construction contract.10

## **Operational Phase: Property Insurance (Building and Contents)**

Upon completion of the renovation and the issuance of the certificate of agibility (*certificato di agibilità*), the insurance requirements transition to the operational phase. For a property held by an S.r.l. and used as a premium short-term rental, a standard homeowner's policy is insufficient.11

### **Building Insurance (Assicurazione Fabbricato)**

Standard building insurance in Italy protects the permanent structure, including walls, foundations, roofs, and fixed installations such as heating and plumbing.13 For the Canadian investment group, the valuation of the building must be based on the "Reconstruction Cost" (*Valore di Ricostruzione*) rather than the market price.14

In premium Italian properties, particularly those in historic centers or rural villas, the reconstruction cost is calculated using a "cubature" method. This involves multiplying the building's volume (cubic meters) by a standardized reconstruction rate, typically ranging from €1,100 to €1,300 per cubic meter, depending on the quality of finishes.15 Failure to insure for the full reconstruction value can trigger the "proportional rule" (*regola proporzionale*), where the insurer reduces a claim payout by the percentage of under-insurance.

### **Specific Risk Extensions**

Beyond fire and explosion, several specific risks are pertinent to the Italian context:

* **Water Damage (Acqua Condotta).** This is the most common cause of claims in Italian residential properties. It covers damage from leaking pipes, bursting boilers, and malfunctioning appliances.16 Given that many renovated villas utilize underfloor heating and high-end plumbing, this coverage should be comprehensive and include the cost of "searching and repairing" the leak, which often involves breaking and replacing expensive tiles.17  
* **Atmospheric Events.** Coverage for windstorms, hail, and snow. For premium properties with gardens, swimming pools, and solar panels, specific riders should be added to ensure these outdoor assets are included.18  
* **Natural Disasters (Earthquake and Flood).** Italy is a high-risk zone for seismic activity. While earthquake coverage is optional and can be expensive, it is highly recommended for properties in central and southern Italy.20 Premiums for earthquake riders are calculated based on the municipality's risk classification and the construction type of the building.22

### **Contents Insurance (Assicurazione Contenuto)**

For a premium short-term rental, the contents represent a significant portion of the total investment. This insurance covers furniture, appliances, artwork, and electronics.13 Standard policies in Italy often provide "Full Replacement Value" (*Valore a Nuovo*), meaning the insurer pays for a new item of similar quality rather than a depreciated sum.23

A critical exclusion to watch for in standard contents policies is the "unoccupancy clause." Many retail policies invalidate coverage if the property is left vacant for more than 45 to 60 consecutive days.13 As the property is intended for short-term rental, there may be periods of vacancy. The S.r.l. must ensure the policy includes an "unoccupancy rider" (*clausola di inabitabilità*) that maintains coverage throughout the year.13

## **Liability Framework for Short-Term Rentals**

Liability is the most significant operational risk for an STR. In Italy, the regulatory framework distinguishes between properties rented by individuals (occasional) and those rented by businesses or through an S.r.l. (professional).24

### **Third-Party Liability (RCT)**

The RCT policy protects the S.r.l. from legal claims arising from its ownership and operation of the property. This is categorized into two parts:

1. **RCT Proprietà (Owner's Liability).** This covers damage caused by the building itself. For example, if a roof tile falls and injures a passerby or if a pipe bursts and ruins a neighbor's antique ceiling.13  
2. **RCT Conduzione (Occupier's Liability).** This covers damage or injury arising from the *use* of the property. For short-term rentals, this is the primary coverage for guest injuries, such as a slip-and-fall on a wet pool deck or an injury caused by a faulty appliance.14

In most Italian regions, holding an RCT policy with a minimum coverage limit (often €500,000 to €1.5 million) is a mandatory requirement for submitting the *Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attività* (SCIA), which is the permit needed to operate a holiday home or B\&B.19

### **Neighborhood Liability (Ricorso Terzi)**

A specific feature of Italian insurance is the *Rischio Locativo* or *Ricorso Terzi*. If a fire starts in the S.r.l.-owned property and spreads to a neighboring building, the owner is liable for the damages to the third party.16 Standard liability policies include this, but for properties in dense historic centers, the limits should be significantly higher—ideally €1.5 million or more—to account for the high density and value of neighboring assets.13

## **Short-Term Rental (STR) Specific Policies: "Affitti Brevi" Products**

The emergence of the "gig economy" in travel has led to the development of specialized Italian insurance products tailored for *Affitti Brevi* (Short-Term Rentals). These policies bridge the gap between residential and commercial insurance, addressing risks that standard home policies specifically exclude.11

### **Guest-Caused Damage and Intentional Acts**

A standard homeowner's policy typically excludes damage caused by paying guests, viewing it as a "business risk".11 Specialized STR policies explicitly cover:

* **Accidental and Intentional Damage.** Protection against guests who damage furniture, break windows, or cause smoke damage in non-smoking properties.29  
* **Theft and Vandalism by Guests.** While standard theft coverage only applies if there is evidence of a break-in, STR policies cover theft of belongings by the guests themselves—a common risk in the luxury segment.11

### **Loss of Rental Income**

If a property becomes uninhabitable due to a covered peril (e.g., a major water leak or fire), the S.r.l. loses its primary revenue stream. An STR-specific policy can include "Loss of Rental Income" (*Lucro Cessante*) coverage.11 This compensates the S.r.l. for the average daily rate it would have earned during the repair period. For a business-owned property, this is vital to cover fixed costs such as mortgage payments and taxes during downtime.12

### **Platform Insurance (AirCover and Vrbo)**

Platforms like Airbnb and Vrbo provide their own host protection programs.11 However, these are secondary insurance products. They only apply to bookings made through those specific platforms and often have restrictive terms, such as requiring a claim to be filed before the next guest checks in.12 A private, comprehensive STR policy issued in Italy is necessary to provide "primary" coverage that complies with local laws and covers bookings from all sources.11

## **Corporate and Residency Considerations: The S.r.l. and Non-Resident Owners**

The use of an S.r.l. as a holding vehicle for the Canadian investors is a standard practice in Italy, providing limited liability and a clear structure for tax and management. However, this structure introduces specific administrative requirements for insurance underwriting.

### **Reciprocity and Legal Basis**

Under the Italian principle of reciprocity, Canadian citizens and companies are permitted to purchase real estate and form companies in Italy because similar rights are granted to Italians in Canada.33 This allows the S.r.l. to be the legal owner and policyholder of all insurance contracts.35

### **Requirements for the Policyholder (The S.r.l.)**

To issue a policy to an S.r.l., Italian insurers will require the following:

* **Partita IVA and Codice Fiscale.** The company's tax identity must be fully registered.35  
* **PEC (Posta Elettronica Certificata).** All official insurance contracts and notices in Italy are now delivered via certified email. The S.r.l. must have an active PEC address.19  
* **Italian Bank Account.** Premiums are typically paid via SEPA direct debit from an Italian bank account. While some international brokers accept transfers, most Italian carriers require local banking for automated renewals.35  
* **Resident Administrator or Representative.** While the shareholders are non-resident, the S.r.l. must have an administrator. If the administrator is also a non-resident, insurers may request a "Local Representative" or a professional property manager to be the point of contact for emergency repairs and site inspections.19

### **Insurance for Non-Resident-Owned SPVs**

Italian insurers (e.g., Generali, Allianz, Unipol) readily cover properties owned by an S.r.l., provided the company is registered in Italy. The residency of the shareholders is generally irrelevant to the underwriting of the *property* risk, but it does affect the *liability* risk.13 Insurers may view a property with no resident occupants as a higher risk for burglary or undetected water leaks, which can lead to higher premiums or a requirement for a connected security and alarm system.13

International and "Expat" brokers like Intasure or Clements are often better equipped to handle the initial communication for non-residents, as they provide documents in English and understand the logistical hurdles of managing a property from abroad.17

### **Deductibles and Limits**

Most Italian policies include a deductible (*franchigia*) of €250 to €500 for standard claims like water damage or theft.13 Increasing the deductible can lead to a 10% to 15% discount on the annual premium.39 For liability, it is recommended to maintain a limit of no less than €1.5 million per occurrence, as bodily injury claims in Italy are calculated based on court-mandated tables (*Tabelle di Milano*) that can lead to high settlements for permanent disability.13

## **Recommended Minimum Coverage List**

For the Canadian investment group, the following coverage "bundle" is considered the industry standard for protecting premium residential assets held through an S.r.l.:

* **Building (Fabbricato):** Full replacement cost with "All-Risks" wording if available. Must include an Earthquake rider.  
* **Third-Party Liability (RCT):** Limit of €1.5 million or €2 million. Must include *RCT Proprietà* and *RCT Conduzione*.  
* **Neighbor Liability (Ricorso Terzi):** Integral to the fire policy; limit should match building value.  
* **Guest Damage:** Specific STR endorsement covering both accidental and malicious damage by guests.  
* **Loss of Rental Income:** Daily indemnity for at least 12 months following a covered loss.  
* **Legal Protection (Tutela Legale):** Independent policy (e.g., through DAS or ARAG) to cover attorney fees for disputes with guests, neighbors, or the building administrator.38  
* **Emergency Assistance:** 24/7 hotline for plumbing, electrical, and locksmith emergencies—essential for non-resident owners.19

## **Emerging Trends and Regulatory Outlook (2025–2026)**

The short-term rental market in Italy is currently undergoing a significant regulatory "unification."

### **The National Identification Code (CIN)**

To combat tax evasion and ensure safety, the Italian government has introduced the CIN (*Codice Identificativo Nazionale*). To obtain a CIN, a property must meet specific safety standards, including the presence of fire extinguishers, carbon monoxide detectors, and—crucially—proof of a compliant liability insurance policy.26 For the S.r.l., this makes professional RCT coverage a mandatory business expense rather than an optional safeguard.

### **Tax Harmonization**

Starting in 2026, the Italian government is proposing to unify the tax rate on short-term rentals at a flat 26%.51 While this primarily affects individual owners under the *Cedolare Secca* regime, it underscores the government’s push to professionalize the sector. For an S.r.l., which is already taxed under the IRES (24%) and IRAP (3.9%) corporate regimes, this change may actually improve the relative competitiveness of corporate ownership compared to casual individual hosting.51

### **Mandatory "Natural Disaster" Insurance**

There is ongoing legislative discussion in Italy regarding the introduction of mandatory insurance for natural disasters (fire, flood, earthquake) for all commercial entities, which would include property-owning S.r.l.s. While not yet law for residential assets, the trend suggests that future premiums will increasingly reflect climate-related risks, necessitating a more proactive approach to property maintenance and site selection.20

## **Conclusion: Integrated Risk Management Strategy**

For a group of Canadian investors holding Italian real estate through an S.r.l., a successful insurance strategy requires a phased approach. The construction phase must be anchored by a robust CAR policy and the statutory Posthumous Decennial warranty, ensuring that the heavy capital investment in renovation is protected from structural defects and site accidents. Upon transitioning to the operational phase as a premium STR, the S.r.l. must move beyond standard homeowner's insurance to a specialized "Affitti Brevi" or "Business Property" policy that explicitly covers guest-caused damage, professional liability, and loss of income. By partnering with an international broker and reputable Italian carriers, the S.r.l. can ensure compliance with regional SCIA requirements and safeguard its long-term profitability against the unique challenges of the Italian tourism market.
